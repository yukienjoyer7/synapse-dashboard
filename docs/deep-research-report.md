# Evidence Map for Smart Hospital Notebook Claims

## Executive summary

For a strong submission, the notebook should anchor its conclusions in a small set of **policy claims that are directly true in Indonesia** and a separate set of **empirical claims that are defensible but should be phrased cautiously**. The strongest Indonesia-specific backbone is: **Permenkes No. 24/2022 on medical records**, **the SATUSEHAT Platform documentation and supporting policies**, **Permenkes No. 3/2020 on hospital classification**, and **the Personal Data Protection Law (UU No. 27/2022)**. Together, these establish that Indonesia expects electronic medical records to be used, exchanged, and governed under a national interoperability framework, with strong privacy obligations. citeturn62view0turn65view3turn12view1turn62view2turn62view1

The strongest empirical framing is more cautious: **digital maturity is useful as a capability benchmark, not a guarantee of outcomes**; **IT spending alone does not ensure successful digitalization**; **telemedicine and e-referral systems often expand access and speed coordination, but utilization and operational effects are context-dependent**; and **BOR/LOS should be interpreted relative to hospital class, case mix, and service mix rather than as universal pass-fail thresholds**. That phrasing is the most evidence-consistent and least vulnerable in judging. citeturn31view0turn32view0turn20academia5turn45academia0turn59search1turn59search0turn58search1turn62view2turn39academia9

## Safer wording for claims with weaker or mixed evidence

The notebook should avoid some common overclaims.

Instead of writing **“Higher IT spending leads to better operational outcomes,”** use: **“Higher digital investment can support better outcomes, but conversion into operational gains depends on governance, workflow redesign, interoperability execution, security compliance, and local IT capability.”** That is much closer to the evidence base and to Indonesia’s actual SATUSEHAT onboarding requirements. citeturn31view0turn59search1turn64view0turn64view3

Instead of writing **“Telemedicine reduces utilization/BOR/LOS,”** use: **“Telemedicine often expands access and can reduce travel and referral friction, but effects on downstream utilization and operational metrics are mixed and highly context-dependent.”** That phrasing is much safer for judges because it matches WHO’s caution and the mixed operational evidence in referral-consult implementations. citeturn32view0turn59search0turn58search1

Instead of writing **“Ideal BOR is X and any deviation is inefficient,”** use: **“BOR and LOS should be interpreted relative to hospital class, service mix, and case mix; sustained very high BOR may indicate capacity strain, while persistently low BOR may indicate underutilization.”** This avoids relying on a brittle universal rule where the evidence base is weaker. citeturn62view2turn39academia9

Instead of writing **“Digital maturity predicts performance,”** use: **“Digital maturity is a capability benchmark associated with better efficiency and data use in some studies, but causal interpretation should be cautious.”** That is the most defensible summary of the current evidence. citeturn20academia5turn45academia0turn31view0

## Citation strategy for the notebook conclusions

For the notebook’s conclusion section, the most robust pattern is to pair **one Indonesia policy source** with **one international evidence source** for each major conclusion. For example: if the notebook concludes that **SatuSehat-connected hospitals should improve referral coordination**, cite the **SATUSEHAT referral playbook** plus **Vimalananda’s e-consult review**. If it concludes that **privacy/security readiness is mandatory before deeper data integration**, cite **UU PDP 27/2022** plus **the SATUSEHAT security-survey requirement**. If it concludes that **digitally weak remote hospitals should be prioritized differently**, cite **WHO’s equity framing** plus the **Reuters report on connectivity expansion for remote Indonesian health facilities**. That pairing gives the conclusion both **local legal legitimacy** and **external empirical credibility**. citeturn51view1turn59search0turn62view1turn64view3turn32view0turn42view0
